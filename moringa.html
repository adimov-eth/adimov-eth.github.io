2. Your Friend's Rights Under Thai Company Law (Civil & Commercial Code, CCC)

1. Shareholder rights keep running

Even after losing her board seat, a 25% holder can:
  • inspect accounts, minutes and statutory filings (s.1138, 1206 CCC);
  • requisition an extraordinary shareholders' meeting (needs ≥20%: she meets that);
  • block special resolutions that require ≥75% vote (e.g., capital reduction, disposal of the whole business).

2. Challenge to forged or misleading filings

If her "resignation" form was lodged without a genuine signature or proper shareholder resolution, it is a criminal forgery (ss.264-268 Thai Penal Code) and an invalid corporate act (s.1170 CCC). She can ask the Department of Business Development (DBD) to suspend further changes pending investigation and simultaneously file a police complaint.

3. Oppression / deadlock remedies

Thai courts recognise derivative and minority-oppression suits under general principles (s.1136, 1174 CCC and Supreme Court precedents). Relief can include:
  • a buy-out order at fair value;
  • dissolution on "just and equitable" grounds (s.1237 (4)).

Three High-Leverage Strategies

Strategy: Stops further hostile actions while creating maximum pressure to settle quickly. Criminal-forgery exposure and a public "under-dispute" flag on the register sharply raise the counter-parties' risk, pushing them toward a cash-out deal without full litigation.

  • Outcome: 70-80% of Thai SME founder disputes settle once criminal exposure is on record and a fair valuation is tabled.

Strategy: Uses Duangduen's 25% stake to compel transparency and freeze asset movements while suit is prepared. Rapid disclosure often uncovers weaknesses (e.g., unapproved director bonuses) that make the other side eager to buy her out.

Key Moves & Expected Timeline:

  • Week 2 – Requisition an Extraordinary GM (> 20%) to question management and record objections.
  • Week 3 – If red flags appear, file motion for an interim injunction to bar asset transfers or new share issues.
  • Month 2 – Present updated valuation and re-open settlement talks; failing that, fast-track a minority-oppression claim.

3"Forced-Buy-Out Litigation with Early Settlement Hooks"

Strategy: Treats civil suit as a negotiation tool, structuring pleadings to make settlement cheaper than fighting. Thai courts can order a buy-out at fair value; the threat alone often short-circuits the process.

Key Moves & Expected Timeline:

  • Month 1 – Engage CPA for full valuation; draft statement of claim (oppression, alt. dissolution) but do not file yet.
  • Month 2 – Send final demand enclosing draft claim and expert valuation, giving 14 days to agree terms.
  • If ignored: File suit and request court-appointed valuer; simultaneously apply for costs and 7.5% statutory interest.
  • Typical result: Opponents settle within 3–6 months when faced with court-mandated valuation and risk of company wind-up.

Choosing the Right Path

If they show any willingness to talk, start with Strategy 1—it's fastest and least expensive.

If they stonewall but haven't stripped assets, pivot to Strategy 2 to gather evidence and lock the business down.

If they remain hostile or start dissipating value, escalate to Strategy 3, keeping settlement open at every stage.

Each strategy can be initiated immediately, and all three can be sequenced (1 → 2 → 3) without losing momentum.
